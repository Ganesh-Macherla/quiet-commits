It gets pretty interesting...neurologically. The human brain doesnt have a dedicated "clock", which means it doesn't actually feel the time passing. There isn't any tiny clock in our heads ticking away. It doesn't measure, the brain instead estimates time...based on two things: where my attention is and where my memories are forming.

When I'm deeply focused...like genuinely studying, coding, building something...my brain puts almost all its attention into that one task only. It stops monitoring everything else, including its assumption of time. That’s why hours can pass and I don’t notice anything. This is a state, we Gen Z peeps call "locking in". But there's a twist to this whole shabang. 

The same thing happens when I'm scrolling brainrot endlessly, eventhough half of the stuff we watch are memes and chaotic internet nonsense, which aren't of any help to the mind and body one bit. That is addcition at it's strongest, but why?. It's not about one's willpower or determination or laziness..It's about how the brain is wired to respond to rewards. Every swipe, gives the brain a tiny reward..a small dopamine hit. Let's get nerdy now (says the guy who was talking shit about nerds on this site earlier..now thats called character development, anyways where was I). Dopamine is a neurotransmitter, basically a chemical messenger the brain uses to signal motivation and reward. It’s mostly produced in areas like the midbrain and released into pathways that reinforce our behavior. And here’s the important part...dopamine isn’t just about pleasure...It’s about anticipation too. And out just brain loves unpredictable rewards, like a dog receiving a chew bone after hours of waiting and neglectance showing through its small teary eyes, suffering because of lack of attention and when it finally gets some, its happy and over the moon, filled with joy after receiveing the tiniest amount of love...NO that got dark, we're not doing that again, anyways.

That is why scrolling stuff online feels so addictive. The next video might be hilarious. The next notification might change my mood. The next swipe might finally be interesting. That uncertainty triggers what psychologists call a variable reward schedule...the same mechanism that makes slot machines (gambling hehe) feel more addictive. Because the reward is unpredictable, the brain releases dopamine in bursts. And every time it does, it strengthens the behavior that led to it. So our attention gets "locked in" again.

Our brain literally starts learning that “scrolling = potential reward”... and it wires that loop tighter each time because our nervous system evolved to chase uncertain rewards...and historically, uncertainty meant opportunity. Opportunity meant survival.
And yes...this traces back to the same survival instincts our hunter-gatherer ancestors relied on.

But this time, the engagement is shallow. We’re stimulated, but we’re not thinking deeply. we’re just reacting..swipe...laugh..swipe..pause...and swipe again.

And while dopamine keeps the loop running, the brain isn’t forming strong, structured memories. Deep thinking activates networks responsible for reasoning, reflection, and long-term memory formation. Rapid scrolling activates just reward circuitry, but it doesn’t give your brain enough sustained focus to encode meaningful experiences. So in the moment, we feel engaged. But in hindsight, there’s nothing solid to remember...that’s why three hours can disappear without feeling real. Our brain wasn’t measuring time. It was infact chasing prediction errors...the tiny gaps between “maybe this is interesting” and “okay, what’s next?”. 

And here’s where it connects to time itself. Our sense of time....depends on memory density. The more meaningful moments our brain records, the longer that period feels. The fewer it records, the more that time compresses. So three hours of deep work feels full and three hours of scrolling feels like it evaporated.
